Dog Breeds


Image Breed Description
Pomeranian Pomeranian has been called "the ideal companion" by many people. Its glorious fur, natural smiling face, and a vivacious personality have helped make them one of the world's most popular breeds
Siberian Husky A thickly coated, sled dog of the right size and great endurance. Siberian Huskies were developed to work in packs, pulling light loads at moderate speeds over vast frozen expanses.
Golden Retriever A Scottish gundog of beauty, it stands among America's most popular dog breeds. They work seriously at hunting and field work, as guides for the blind people, and in search-and-rescue.
Afghan Hound These dogs are well known for their elegant beauty. The thick, silky, and soft coat is the breed's crowning glory it is used as a protection from the harsh climate.
Chihuahua The Chihuahua is a tiny dog with a huge personality. A national symbol of Mexico, these alert and amusing "purse dogs" stand among the oldest breeds of the Americas, with a lineage going back to the ancient kingdoms of pre-Columbian times.
Bulldog Kind but courageous, friendly but dignified, the Bulldog is a thick-set, low-slung, well-muscled bruiser whose 'sourmug'face is the universal symbol of courage and tenacity. These docile, loyal companions adapt well to town or country.
Poodle The Poodle is the national dog of France, and the French sure do love their Poodles. There is, however, no such breed as the “French Poodle.” In France, Poodles are known as the Caniche, or “duck dog.”
American Eskimo Dog The American Eskimo Dog combines striking good looks with a quick and clever mind in a total brains-and-beauty package. Neither shy nor aggressive, Eskies are always alert and friendly, though a bit conservative when making new friends.
Rottweiler The Rottweiler is a robust working breed of great strength descended from the mastiffs of the Roman legions. A gentle playmate and protector within the family circle, the Rottie observes the outside world with a self-assured aloofness.
Dachshund “Dachshund” is a German word meaning “badger dog,” and the breed’s German history goes back some 600 years. And, as the breed name suggests, the Dachshund was developed to enthusiastically dig his way into a badger den and dispatch its occupant.
